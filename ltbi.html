Latent Tuberculosis Infection (LTBI)

10 Marks ~1000 Words Respiratory Medicine

Definition

Latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI) is defined as a state of persistent immune response to stimulation by Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) antigens without evidence of clinically manifested active TB disease — i.e., no symptoms, no radiographic abnormality, and no bacteriological evidence of active disease.

Key Distinction: LTBI ≠ Active TB. Persons with LTBI are not infectious. They harbour viable but dormant bacilli within granulomas. The lifetime risk of progression to active TB is 5–15%, with the majority occurring within the first 2–5 years of initial infection.

Epidemiology

  • Approximately one-third of the world's population is estimated to be infected with Mtb
  • In India, nearly 40% of the population is latently infected; annual risk of TB infection is ~1.5%
  • India accounts for ~25% of global incident TB cases
  • Of those with LTBI, only 5–15% develop active TB over a lifetime
  • Risk of progression is highest in the first 2 years after initial infection
  • Currently available LTBI treatment regimens have efficacy ranging from 60–90%, with protection lasting up to 19 years
  • WHO End TB Strategy targets: 90% reduction in TB incidence and 95% reduction in TB deaths by 2035

Pathophysiology of Latency

  • After primary infection, Mtb is contained within granulomas by host T-cell mediated immunity (Th1 response)
  • Within caseous centre: low oxygen tension + low pH + fatty acid accumulation → bacteria downregulate metabolism (DosR regulon activation)
  • Bacilli enter a non-replicating persistent state — metabolically dormant but viable
  • Viable bacilli may persist for decades even in calcified (Ranke complex) lesions
  • Reactivation is triggered when host immunity is impaired:
    • CD4⁺ T-cell depletion (HIV)
    • Anti-TNF therapy — disrupts granuloma integrity
    • Immunosuppressants, malnutrition, diabetes, ageing
  • Liquefaction of caseum → cavity formation → massive bacillary load → active disease

High-Risk Groups for LTBI Testing & Treatment (WHO Prioritisation)

Priority Groups — Mandatory Screening

  • People Living with HIV (PLHIV) — all adults, adolescents, children regardless of ART status; risk of progression is 20–30× higher than immunocompetent individuals
  • Household contacts of bacteriologically confirmed pulmonary TB:
    • Children <5 years — highest risk of rapid progression to severe/disseminated disease
    • Children >5 years, adolescents, adults — in high-incidence settings, "may" receive preventive treatment
  • Patients initiating anti-TNF therapy
  • Patients on dialysis
  • Patients preparing for organ or haematological transplant
  • Patients with silicosis

Additional Groups (Low-Incidence Countries)

  • Prisoners, healthcare workers, immigrants from high-burden countries
  • Homeless persons, illicit drug users
NOT recommended for systematic LTBI screening: People with diabetes, harmful alcohol use, tobacco smokers, or underweight persons — unless they are already in the above high-risk categories.

Programmatic Management Algorithm (WHO Cascade of Care)

Step 1: Identify & Prioritise High-Risk Groups PLHIV · Household contacts · Anti-TNF · Dialysis · Transplant · Silicosis
↓
Step 2: Rule Out Active TB Disease Symptom screen (cough, fever, weight loss, night sweats) + Chest X-ray ± NAAT/Smear/Culture
↓
IF SYMPTOMS PRESENT → Investigate for Active TB | IF NO SYMPTOMS → Proceed
Step 3: Test for LTBI TST (Mantoux) or IGRA (QuantiFERON-TB Gold / T-SPOT.TB)
↓
✓ Positive TST/IGRA
Chest X-ray to rule out active disease → if normal → Initiate LTBI treatment
✗ Negative TST/IGRA
High-risk contacts (children <5, immunocompromised) → still offer LTBI treatment after ruling out active TB
↓
Step 4: Initiate & Complete LTBI Treatment Choose regimen based on age, HIV status, drug-resistance of index case, hepatotoxicity risk
↓
Step 5: Monitor — Adverse Events, Adherence, Completion Follow-up for development of active TB during and after LTBI treatment completion

Method Intradermal injection; induration read at 48–72 h In vitro blood test; IFN-γ measured by ELISA/ELISPOT

BCG interference False positives due to prior BCG (unless given at birth only) No cross-reactivity with BCG
NTM cross-reactivity More cross-reactive with NTM Less cross-reactive with NTM
Sensitivity in HIV Reduced (anergy) Reduced (anergy)
Cost Less expensive; no laboratory needed More expensive; requires laboratory

Active TB diagnosis? NEITHER TST NOR IGRA should be used to diagnose active TB — for LTBI screening only
TST Positivity Thresholds (India/WHO): ≥5 mm in HIV-infected, recent TB contacts, immunosuppressed patients; ≥10 mm in other high-risk groups; ≥15 mm in persons with no known risk factors.

Preventive Treatment for Contacts of DR-TB Cases

  • Standard INH-based regimens are ineffective for contacts of INH-resistant or MDR-TB cases
  • Management requires individualised risk assessment based on DST profile of index case
  • Strict clinical observation for ≥2 years is preferred over empirical preventive treatment for MDR-TB contacts

Monitoring of Adverse Effects

  • Isoniazid: Asymptomatic liver enzyme elevation, peripheral neuropathy (give pyridoxine 10–25 mg/day), hepatotoxicity (potentially fatal — 1% in elderly)
  • Rifampicin/Rifapentine: Cutaneous reactions, gastrointestinal intolerance, hepatotoxicity, drug interactions (especially with ART)
  • Baseline LFTs required in: chronic liver disease, alcohol use, HIV, age >35 years, pregnancy and postpartum
  • Drug-resistance risk with LTBI treatment → prevented by:
    • Ensuring active TB is ruled out before starting
    • Strict monitoring and patient education
    • Avoiding partial treatment of active TB masquerading as LTBI

LTBI Management in India — Special Considerations

  • India bears ~25% of global TB burden; 40% of population latently infected
  • Treating 40% of the population for LTBI is neither rational nor practicable
  • NTEP priority remains treating smear-positive active TB to interrupt transmission
  • LTBI treatment in India should focus on targeted high-risk groups only:
    • HIV-infected individuals
    • Juvenile contacts of sputum-positive cases with recent TST conversion
    • Those on immunosuppressants/corticosteroids/anti-TNF agents
    • Patients with silicosis, renal failure, planned transplant
  • Important warning: TST and IGRA are being misused for active TB diagnosis in India — they should NOT be used for active TB diagnosis in high-endemic settings (STCI guidelines)
  • In India, IGRA should not replace TST; TST is preferred in public health settings

Efficacy of LTBI Regimens vs Placebo (WHO 2015 Meta-analysis)

Regimen OR for Incident TB (vs Placebo) Quality of Evidence Hepatotoxicity vs Placebo
Isoniazid 6 months 0.61 (0.48–0.77) — 39% risk reduction Low OR 0.99 (no significant difference)
Isoniazid 12–72 months 0.53 (0.41–0.69) — 47% risk reduction Low OR 0.59 (slightly less)
Rifampicin 3–4 months 0.48 (0.26–0.87) — 52% risk reduction Moderate Significantly less hepatotoxicity than INH
Rifampicin + INH 3–4 months 0.52 (0.33–0.84) — 48% risk reduction Low Similar to INH alone
3HP (weekly RPT+INH) vs 9H INH 0.44 (0.18–1.07) — non-inferior Low OR 0.16 — significantly less hepatotoxicity
Key Conclusions from Trials: No single regimen is clearly superior for efficacy. However, for safety: Rifampicin 3–4 months and 3-month weekly Rifapentine+INH have significantly fewer hepatotoxicity events. For adherence: 3HP (12 weekly doses) has highest completion rates. For HIV: at least 36 months INH is recommended in high-burden settings.

Conclusion: LTBI represents the reservoir from which future TB cases will emerge. Targeted identification and treatment of LTBI in high-risk groups — particularly PLHIV, child contacts, and immunocompromised individuals — is a cornerstone of the WHO End TB Strategy. In India, a pragmatic, focused approach is essential given the enormous burden of latent infection and limited resources.
