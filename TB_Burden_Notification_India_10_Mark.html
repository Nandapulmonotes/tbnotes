Introduction

  • Tuberculosis (TB) is an ancient disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, remains the leading infectious disease killer globally after COVID-19
  • TB is a notifiable disease in India under the Epidemic Diseases Act; mandatory notification introduced universally in 2012
  • India carries the highest TB burden in the world — 26% of all global TB cases (WHO Global TB Report 2022)
  • India's Prime Minister declared TB elimination by 2025 — five years ahead of the global End TB target of 2030
  • National TB Elimination Programme (NTEP) — formerly RNTCP — is India's national programme overseeing TB control and notification
India's Ambition "TB Harega, Desh Jitega" — India aims to eliminate TB (incidence <1 per 1,00,000 population) by 2025, a full 5 years before the WHO End TB Strategy deadline of 2030

  • WHO maintains three high-burden country lists — for TB, TB/HIV, and MDR-TB
  • India features on all three WHO high-burden lists
  • India is among 30 high-burden TB countries globally (accounting for 87% of global TB burden)

Critical Gap — The Missing Million Every year, a large proportion of estimated TB cases are never diagnosed or not notified — the "missing cases". In 2022, India notified 24.2 lakh (2.42 million) cases against an estimated 28 lakh (2.8 million) — leaving ~3.8 lakh cases unaccounted for. These untreated cases fuel ongoing transmission.

Risk Factors for TB in India

🦠
HIV Co-infection
HIV is the strongest single risk factor; increases TB reactivation risk 20–30× above background; 5.7% of TB patients in India are HIV+
🍽️
Undernutrition
BMI <18.5 kg/m² is the largest population-attributable risk factor for TB in India; accounts for ~55% of India's TB burden. India has the highest global burden of undernutrition-associated TB
🚬
Tobacco Smoking
Smoking doubles TB incidence and triples TB mortality; India has 270 million tobacco users; ~25% of TB cases attributable to tobacco
🍶
Diabetes Mellitus
DM increases TB risk 2–3×; India has 77 million diabetics; DM-TB "double burden". TB-DM patients have worse treatment outcomes
🏘️
Overcrowding / Poverty
Urban slums, dense housing, poor ventilation drive airborne transmission. Socioeconomic deprivation is a fundamental TB determinant
💊
Immunosuppression
Corticosteroid use, anti-TNF therapy, organ transplant, renal failure, malignancy — all increase TB risk. Steroids/anti-TNF: 5–10× increased risk
Nikshay Poshan Yojana (NPY) To address undernutrition, India provides ₹500/month direct benefit transfer to all TB patients during treatment under Nikshay Poshan Yojana — recognising that nutritional support is integral to TB cure

Evolution of TB Control in India: RNTCP → NTEP

1962
National TB Programme (NTP) launched — first systematic TB control effort; district TB centres established; limited success due to poor infrastructure
1992
Revised National TB Control Programme (RNTCP) piloted — adopted WHO-recommended DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) strategy; phased national rollout from 1997
2006
RNTCP achieves national coverage; DOTS extended to entire country; WHO-declared milestones (70% case detection, 85% cure rate) achieved ahead of schedule
2012
Mandatory TB notification made statutory under Gazette Notification — ALL healthcare providers (public and private) required to notify TB cases. TB declared a notifiable disease under Epidemic Diseases Act
2015
Nikshay portal (IT-based TB case management system) launched for real-time online notification; WHO End TB Strategy adopted globally (targets: 2030)
2017
PM's commitment to TB elimination by 2025; National Strategic Plan (NSP) 2017–25 launched with "Detect-Treat-Prevent-Build" strategy; daily ATT replaces thrice-weekly regimen
2020
RNTCP renamed to National TB Elimination Programme (NTEP) — signalling shift from "control" to "elimination"; free molecular diagnostics (CBNAAT/TrueNat) scaled up at block level
2022–25
India TB Report 2023: 24.2 lakh notifications (highest ever); Pradhan Mantri TB Mukt Bharat Abhiyaan (PMTBMBA) launched — community-level TB elimination push; universal DST target; 100-day intensified campaign

TB Notification System — Nikshay

What is Nikshay? Nikshay (meaning "to eliminate" in Sanskrit) is India's web-based IT platform for TB case-based surveillance, notification, treatment follow-up, and benefits delivery. URL: nikshay.in

  • Legal mandate: TB notification became statutory under the Gazette Notification of 2012 (under Epidemic Diseases Act 1897) — mandatory for all healthcare providers including private sector
  • Nikshay ID: Each notified patient receives a unique Nikshay ID for tracking across the treatment cascade
  • Private sector engagement: Private providers notifying through PPSA (Public-Private Support Agency), PP-DOTS, and direct Nikshay portal entry
  • Nikshay Poshan Yojana (NPY): ₹500/month Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) to all TB patients' bank accounts for nutritional support
  • Nikshay Mitra: Launched 2022 — citizens, NGOs, corporates "adopt" TB patients to provide support beyond medication
  • Incentive for notifiers: ₹500 one-time incentive to private providers for notifying a TB patient (PMTBMBA scheme)

  • Presumptive TB cases = persons with symptoms or signs suggestive of TB (previously called "TB suspects")
  • Bacteriologically confirmed TB = sputum smear-positive, culture-positive, or CBNAAT/Xpert MTB-positive
  • Clinically diagnosed TB = diagnosed by clinician based on CXR, histology, or clinical grounds without bacteriological confirmation

  • States with highest TB notifications (India TB Report 2023): Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat

End TB Strategy & India's NSP 2017–25

WHO End TB Strategy Targets

  • By 2025: 75% reduction in TB deaths from 2015 baseline; 50% reduction in incidence
  • By 2030: 90% reduction in TB deaths; 80% reduction in incidence (SDG target)
  • By 2035: 95% reduction in deaths; 90% reduction in incidence (TB elimination = <1/1,00,000)
  • Zero catastrophic costs for TB-affected households by 2020
  • Three pillars: Integrated patient-centred care · Bold policies · Intensified research

India NSP 2017–25: "DTPB" Framework

  • D — Detect: Universal CBNAAT/TrueNat; AI-enabled CXR screening; molecular diagnostics at block level
  • T — Treat: Daily ATT; universal DST; bedaquiline + delamanid for MDR-XDR; adherence support via 99DOTS
  • P — Prevent: IPT for PLHIV; TB preventive therapy (TPT) for contacts; BCG vaccination; airborne infection control
  • B — Build: Strengthen health systems; digital tools (Nikshay 2.0); private sector engagement; social protection

  • Universal DST: All diagnosed TB patients to undergo drug susceptibility testing — shifting from syndromic to precision treatment

  • New drugs for DR-TB: Bedaquiline, Delamanid, Pretomanid — BPaL / BPaLC regimens replacing injectables; all-oral MDR-TB regimens now standard

Challenges & Summary

Key Challenges in TB Elimination

  • Large "missing cases" gap (~3–4 lakh/year undetected or unreported)
  • High private sector TB treatment without notification (estimated 50% of TB treated privately)
  • Drug-resistant TB — MDR-TB treatment success only 58–60%
  • Undernutrition — largest population-attributable risk factor
  • COVID-19 setbacks — 25% drop in notifications in 2020
  • TB-DM dual burden — worsening treatment outcomes
  • Stigma and late presentation — especially in women
  • Inadequate contact tracing and TPT uptake

Key Achievements (NTEP)

  • 24.22 lakh notifications in 2022 — highest ever
  • 86–87% treatment success rate for DS-TB
  • Universal free CBNAAT diagnostics at district level
  • Nikshay Poshan Yojana — ₹2,000+ crore DBT to TB patients
  • Free bedaquiline/delamanid for all MDR/XDR-TB
  • Private sector: ~50% of notifications now from private sector
  • Paediatric TB — improved diagnosis with molecular tools
  • 99DOTS — digital adherence monitoring scaled nationally
