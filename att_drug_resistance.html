2. Mechanisms of Development of Drug Resistance

Epidemiological context (WHO Global TB Report 2024): India contributes 27% of global MDR/RR-TB burden. MDR-TB proportion: ~2.5% new cases; ~13% previously treated cases.

2.1 Types of Drug Resistance

  • Mono-resistance: Resistance to one FLD only
  • Poly-resistance: Resistance to >1 FLD, but not both H and R
  • MDR-TB: Resistance to both H and R (± others)
  • Pre-XDR-TB: MDR/RR-TB + resistance to any fluoroquinolone (Lfx or Mfx)
  • XDR-TB: Pre-XDR-TB + resistance to ≥1 Group A drug (Bdq or Lzd or both)
  • RR-TB: Any resistance to R (includes MDR, XDR, mono-R)

2.2 Fundamental Basis: Selection of Pre-existing Mutants

M. tuberculosis does not acquire new mutations in response to drugs — resistance emerges by selective amplification of spontaneously occurring mutants already present in the mycobacterial population.

Natural mutation rates:
  • INH resistance (katG): ~1 in 10⁶ organisms
  • RIF resistance (rpoB): ~1 in 10⁸ organisms
  • Dual resistance (MDR): ~1 in 10¹⁴ — extremely rare de novo; thus MDR usually emerges stepwise

2.4 Molecular Mechanisms of Resistance

Each drug has specific target genes whose mutation confers resistance:

Drug Resistance Gene(s) Encoded Protein/Mechanism Notes
INH (H) katG, inhA, kasA, ahpC katG: catalase-peroxidase (activates INH prodrug); inhA: enoyl-ACP reductase (InhA overexpression = low-level resistance) katG Ser315Thr — most common; inhA promoter → cross-resistance to Eto
RIF (R) rpoB β-subunit of RNA polymerase; mutations in 81-bp RRDR (Rifampicin Resistance Determining Region) rpoB Ser531Leu/His most common; surrogate for MDR-TB; detected by GeneXpert
PZA (Z) pncA Pyrazinamidase converts PZA → pyrazinoic acid (active form); mutations inactivate enzyme Most mutations unique per strain; heterogeneous
EMB (E) embB (embCAB operon) Arabinosyl transferase mutations (embB Met306 most frequent) Lower-level resistance; EMB intrinsically less potent
FQ (Lfx/Mfx) gyrA, gyrB DNA gyrase mutations in QRDR; GyrA Asp94Gly/Ala/His most frequent Detected by SL-LPA; stepwise mutations increase MIC
Bedaquiline (Bdq) atpE; Rv0678 (mmpR) atpE: direct target (ATP synthase c-subunit); Rv0678: MmpS5-MmpL5 efflux pump overexpression Rv0678 also confers clofazimine cross-resistance
Linezolid (Lzd) rrl (23S rRNA), rplC 23S rRNA mutations in peptidyl transferase centre; rplC: ribosomal protein L3 mutation Low-level resistance emerging with prolonged use
Pretomanid (Pa) fbiA/B/C, ddn, fgd1 Mutations in bioreductive activation pathway (deazaflavin-dependent nitroreductase Ddn) Cross-resistance with Delamanid possible

2.5 Biochemical Categories of Resistance Mechanisms

① Target Site Mutation
  • Mutation in drug's binding site protein
  • Drug cannot bind effectively
  • rpoB → RIF resistance
  • gyrA/B → FQ resistance
  • atpE → Bedaquiline resistance
② Prodrug Activation Failure
  • Several ATT drugs are prodrugs requiring activation
  • katG inactivation → INH not converted to active form
  • pncA mutation → PZA not activated to pyrazinoic acid
  • ddn/fbiABC → Pretomanid/Delamanid not activated
③ Drug Efflux Pumps
  • Upregulation of efflux transporters
  • Rv1258c (Tap), MmpL5 (Rv0676c)
  • Lowers intracellular drug concentration
  • Rv0678 → efflux of Bdq + Cfz (cross-resistance)
  • Intrinsic: mycolic acid barrier lowers permeability (Brennan & Nikaido)
④ Target Overexpression
  • Drug present, but overwhelmed by excess target
  • inhA promoter mutation → overexpression of InhA
  • Low-level INH resistance + cross-resistance to Eto
  • Clinically relevant for regimen design (InhA vs. KatG)
⑤ Heteroresistance & Tolerance
  • Mixed population: susceptible + resistant subpopulations
  • Persister cells: phenotypically drug-tolerant (metabolically dormant)
  • Precursor to genotypic resistance (Sotgiu, Dheda et al., Lancet Respir Med)
  • Relevant to treatment failure and relapse
⑥ Compensatory Mutations
  • Secondary mutations restore bacterial fitness
  • rpoC, rpoA mutations restore rpoB-mutant fitness
  • Allows fit MDR strains to transmit efficiently (Comas et al., Nat Genet)
  • GWAS: Sandgren et al., Farhat et al. — epistasis demonstrated

4. Intrinsic vs. Acquired Resistance

Feature Intrinsic Resistance Acquired Resistance
Definition Natural, pre-existing resistance before drug exposure Develops during/after drug exposure
Mechanism Mycolic acid-rich cell wall (low permeability), natural efflux pumps Selection of resistant mutants under drug pressure
Example M.tb intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics (penicillins, macrolides) — Brennan & Nikaido rpoB mutation under RIF monotherapy
Clinical relevance Limits treatment options; guides regimen design Results from inadequate therapy — preventable

5. Contributory Factors to Resistance Development

Patient-Level Factors
  • Poor adherence to treatment
  • Default / treatment interruption
  • Substance abuse, malnutrition (↓ drug absorption)
  • HIV co-infection (↑ pill burden, interactions)
Clinician/Programme Factors
  • Incorrect regimen / dose
  • Addition of single drug to failing regimen
  • Monotherapy (DOTS failure context)
  • Failure to detect FQ resistance before prescribing
Drug/Supply Factors
  • Substandard drug quality
  • Drug shortages / irregular supply
  • Incorrect storage
Bacterial Factors
  • High mutation rate in specific loci
  • Heteroresistance in mixed populations
  • Persister/tolerant phenotype in necrotic granulomas
  • Beijing lineage — higher fitness costs offset by compensatory mutations (ICMR-NTI data)

6. Detection of Drug Resistance: Diagnostic Approach (NTEP 2024)

  • NAAT (GeneXpert MTB/RIF): Detects rpoB mutations → rapid RIF resistance detection (first-line screening for all presumptive TB)
  • CBNAAT M.tb/XDR test: Rapid detection of H, FQ, SLI, and Eto resistance (introduced Aug 2024 by NTEG)
  • FL-LPA (First-Line Line Probe Assay): Detects katG, inhA → H resistance; rpoB → R resistance
  • SL-LPA (Second-Line LPA): Detects gyrA/B → FQ resistance; rrs/eis → SLI resistance
  • Liquid Culture + DST (MGIT 960): Gold standard — phenotypic susceptibility; required for Bdq, Lzd, Pa, Dlm DST
  • tNGS (targeted Next Generation Sequencing): Comprehensive resistance profiling; enables detection of emerging resistance patterns
  • WHO Mutation Catalogue v2 (2023): Definitive mutation–resistance correlations with graded confidence for all 1st and 2nd line drugs
KEY PRINCIPLE: Resistance to one drug at a time (stepwise) → never add a single drug to a failing regimen — this is the classic "amplifier" scenario leading to MDR-TB

7. Implications for Regimen Design (BPaLM Era)

  • BPaLM (Bedaquiline + Pretomanid + Linezolid + Moxifloxacin) — 4 drugs with distinct molecular targets minimize chance of pre-existing multiply-resistant mutants
  • Moxifloxacin retained in BPaLM even with FQ resistance — TB-PRACTECAL data shows BPaLM superior to BPaL (89% vs 77% success); 94% sustained success at 108 weeks even in FQ-resistant cases
  • Lzd resistance emerging concern → structured dose reduction protocol; monitoring rrl/rplC mutations at follow-up
  • Rv0678 mutations → simultaneous Bdq + Cfz resistance — explains use of tNGS baseline DST in India
  • INH mutation pattern (katG vs. inhA) determines: katG → high-level resistance (Hh not useful) vs. inhA → low-level (Eto cross-resistance; Hh may still be effective)
Examination Tip: Always link mutation type to clinical decision — inhA mutation means Eto is contraindicated in 9–11 month shorter oral regimen (replace with Lzd). katG mutation means Hh is ineffective.
Summary of Key Molecular Targets:
  • Cell wall synthesis: INH (katG/inhA), EMB (embB), Pa/Dlm (fbiA/ddn)
  • Transcription: RIF (rpoB)
  • Translation: Streptomycin (rrs, rpsL), Lzd (rrl, rplC)
  • DNA replication: FQ (gyrA/B)
  • Energy metabolism: PZA (pncA), Bdq (atpE)
  • Efflux: Bdq/Cfz (Rv0678-MmpL5), multiple (Rv1258c)
