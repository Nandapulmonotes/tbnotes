Lower Lung Field Tuberculosis

Ahluwalia & Sharma | KS Phaneendra Kumar | Textbook of Tuberculosis

📌 Definition & Terminology
Lower Lung Field (LLF): Area on the PA chest radiograph extending below an imaginary horizontal line traced across the hila, including parahilar regions.
  • LLF-TB = Tuberculous involvement of lower lung fields without simultaneous upper lobe involvement
  • LLF includes: Right Middle Lobe (RML), Lingula (left), and bilateral lower lobes
  • Classical adult TB affects apical/posterior segments of upper lobes and superior segments of lower lobes — LLF-TB is thus an atypical presentation
  • Synonyms used in literature: Basal TB, Lower lobe TB, Hilar/Perihilar TB
  • Ossen classified cases as:
    • Pure group – No visible lesions in upper lung fields
    • Impure group – Nodular or fibrotic infiltrations in one or both apices
  • First described by Kidd (1886); Fowler (1888) stated apex of lower lobe is "second in vulnerability to the apex itself"
📊 Prevalence / Incidence
  • Wide variation: 0.003% to 18.3% across studies — due to differences in terminology and selection bias
  • Indian studies report higher prevalence than Western studies (due to tight waist clothing → restricted diaphragm movement)

🔬 Pathogenesis

Why does TB affect lower lobes? — Mechanisms

  • Primary mechanism: Ulceration of a bronchus by a TB-affected lymph node → spillage of bacilli into lower lobe bronchi → LLF disease as a continuum of primary TB or in early post-primary phase
  • Restricted ventilation: Tight abdominal clothing (sari, dhoti) → impaired diaphragmatic movement → costal breathing → decreased ventilation & lymphatic flow in lower zones
  • Retrograde lymphatic flow from involved hilar nodes
  • Haematogenous dissemination from primary pulmonary/lymph node foci → granulomatous lesions in lower zones
  • Middle & lower lobes receive most of inspired air (especially in upright position) → accessible to inhaled bacilli in primary infection
  • LLF-TB is more common in females — possibly due to tight dressing compressing the lower chest → restricted ventilation

⚠ High-risk groups for LLF-TB: Diabetes mellitus · HIV/AIDS · Chronic kidney disease · Chronic liver disease · Corticosteroid therapy · Pregnancy · Silicosis · Kyphoscoliosis · Transplant recipients
  • Diabetes & LLF-TB:
    • LLF-TB more common at presentation in diabetics
    • Higher AFB smear positivity in diabetics vs non-diabetics
    • Poor glycaemic control (↑ HbA1c) → ↑ relative risk of LLF-TB
    • Diabetics with TB: elderly, higher mortality, poor treatment outcome
🩺 Clinical Features

Epidemiology

  • Female preponderance noted in most studies
  • Previously a disease of young (<40 years — Segarra: 89%; Parmar: 46% <20 yrs); now increasingly affects elderly
  • Mean symptom duration: 12 weeks; most present within 6 months
  • Tripathy & Nanda: 20% reported within 2 weeks; 70% within 6 months

Symptoms

Respiratory

  • Cough (most frequent — 100% in Mathur et al)
  • Expectoration (variable)
  • Haemoptysis — prominent; Tripathy & Nanda: ~67%; Romendick: 75%
  • More frequent with cavitary disease

Systemic (Toxaemic)

  • Fever, chills, malaise
  • Anorexia, weight loss
  • Night sweats
  • Lack of fever >38°C — discriminating feature vs bacterial pneumonia (East Asian study)
Clinical clue: Non-resolving chest shadow + persistent fever >2 weeks + failure to respond to broad-spectrum antibiotics → suspect LLF-TB

Signs

  • Physical signs vary with extent of lesion
  • Small lesions (especially apical segment of lower lobe) → scanty or absent signs
  • Physical signs encountered more often in LLF-TB than in classical upper lobe TB
  • Extensive involvement → pronounced signs: dullness on percussion, bronchial breathing, crepitations
🔭 Investigations

A. Chest Radiograph (PA View)

  • Right lung involved in >50% cases; left ~1/3; bilateral ~10%
  • Right side predominance: RML + right anterobasal segments most common
  • Radiographic findings differ significantly from upper lobe TB — often mimic bacterial/viral pneumonia

  • Pleural effusion: More frequent in LLF-TB than upper lobe TB
  • Atelectasis or solitary mass with intrathoracic lymphadenopathy
  • Absence of air bronchogram — more common in LLF-TB vs bacterial pneumonia
  • Prognostic value: Lung collapse / consolidation on CXR → unfavourable outcome

B. Sputum Examination

  • ZN staining (AFB smear) — cheap, easily available; often negative in LLF-TB
  • Diagnostic yield better in cavitary disease
  • Induced sputum with 3% normal saline → improves yield
  • Xpert MTB/RIF and RT-PCR → increased diagnosis; preferred over smear alone

C. Fibreoptic Bronchoscopy (FOB) — Preferred Modality

  • Higher diagnostic yield than sputum, especially in consolidation, collapse, solitary mass
  • Samples: Bronchial wash, BAL, bronchial biopsy, TBLB
  • Abnormal FOB findings in LLF-TB:
    • Ulcerative granuloma
    • Mucosal erythema
    • Submucosal infiltration
    • Fibrostenosis (worst prognosis)
  • Diagnosis of endobronchial disease: bronchoscopic stenosis / severe tracheobronchitis / roentgenographic atelectasis / tension cavities

D. EBUS-TBNA (Endobronchial Ultrasound – Transbronchial Needle Aspiration)

  • Useful in mediastinal/hilar lymphadenopathy + smear-negative TB suspects
  • FOB + EBUS (China study):
    • AFB smear in BAL: 31.5% vs 12.5% (FOB alone), p=0.018
    • MTB culture in BAL: 67.1% vs 47.9%, p=0.024
    • Overall diagnostic accuracy: 80.8% vs 8.3%, p=0.035
  • EBUS-TBNA → higher yield in pulmonary TB suspects with lymphadenopathy

E. CT Thorax

  • Features: Cavities, tree-in-bud pattern, endobronchial spread, consolidation, mediastinal lymphadenopathy
  • Aids early and more frequent diagnosis of LLF-TB

⚖️ Differential Diagnosis

Common DDx

  • Non-resolving bacterial pneumonia ⭐
  • Lung malignancy / carcinoma
  • Lung abscess
  • Bronchiectasis

Less Common DDx

  • Fungal infections (aspergillosis)
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD)
  • Foreign body aspiration (children)
  • Pulmonary infarction
Key differentiating point: LLF-TB — lack of fever >38°C is more common vs bacterial pneumonia; absence of air bronchogram on CXR favours LLF-TB
💊 Treatment & Management

Medical (Anti-TB Therapy — ATT)

  • Treatment is similar to pulmonary TB elsewhere
  • Standard regimen: 2HRZE / 4HRE (Intensive 2 months + Continuation 4 months)
  • Patients show favourable response to conventional ATT
  • Delayed diagnosis worsens outcome — diagnosis-to-treatment time <3 months → significant CXR resolution and sputum negativity
  • India: Patients receive DOTS under NTEP (erstwhile RNTCP)
  • Long-term follow-up recommended to detect relapse

Bronchoscopic Interventions

  • May be needed to clear endobronchial obstruction: mucus impaction, lymph nodes blocking bronchi
  • Close FOB follow-up if no CXR improvement or worsening endobronchial lesions

Surgical Intervention

  • Indicated if re-examination with FOB shows no improvement or worsening of endobronchial involvement
  • Severe fibrostenosis → early sleeve operation before permanent sequelae (lung damage distal to obstruction, respiratory failure)

Complications to Watch

  • Tracheobronchial stenosis — important complication → permanent lung damage distal to obstruction
  • Flow-volume loops and CXR are insensitive for monitoring stenosis → FOB is gold standard
  • FOB findings of fibrostenosis or ulcerative granuloma → unfavourable prognosis → close follow-up mandatory
🧠 Quick Recall — High-Yield Points
  • LLF-TB: Below imaginary line across hila on PA CXR; includes RML, lingula, lower lobes
  • Most common pathogenic mechanism: TB lymph node ulceration into bronchus
  • High-risk: DM, HIV, CKD, CLD, steroids, pregnancy
  • Clinical hallmark: Non-resolving pneumonia not responding to broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • CXR: Confluent consolidation > patchy consolidation > cavitation; right side > left side
  • Absence of air bronchogram → favours LLF-TB over bacterial pneumonia
  • Diagnosis: FOB is preferred — higher yield than sputum; EBUS-TBNA for lymphadenopathy
  • FOB worst prognostic signs: Fibrostenosis & Ulcerative granuloma
  • Treatment: Standard 2HRZE/4HRE; early diagnosis (<3 months) → better outcome
  • Surgical: Sleeve operation for severe fibrostenosis
Remember: LLF-TB is a masquerader — it mimics pneumonia, malignancy, bronchiectasis and lung abscess. A high index of suspicion in the right clinical context (DM, elderly, non-resolving pneumonia) is the key to early diagnosis.
