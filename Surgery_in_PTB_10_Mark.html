Introduction & Historical Perspective

  • Surgery was the primary treatment for TB in the pre-chemotherapy era (before 1950s) — collapse therapy, pneumothorax, and thoracoplasty were mainstays
  • With the advent of effective anti-TB drugs (Streptomycin 1944, INH 1952, Rifampicin 1963), the role of surgery dramatically declined from primary to adjunctive
  • Today, surgery plays a complementary, adjunctive role — reserved for specific complications, treatment failures, and drug-resistant TB
  • The rise of MDR-TB and XDR-TB has renewed interest in surgery as a potentially curative adjunct when chemotherapy alone fails
  • Surgery in TB is always an adjunct to chemotherapy — never a substitute; optimal ATT before and after surgery is mandatory
Core Principle Surgery in PTB = "Last resort for the complications of TB, not a treatment for TB itself." Anti-TB drugs remain the cornerstone; surgery addresses anatomical consequences that drugs cannot reverse — cavities, destroyed lobes, bronchiectasis, haemoptysis, empyema.

Role of Surgery — Overview

🩺
Diagnostic
When other methods fail
Open lung biopsy / VATS biopsy for diagnosis when bronchoscopy, CT-guided biopsy, and sputum studies are non-diagnostic. Mediastinoscopy / EBUS-TBNA for lymph node sampling
🔪
Therapeutic — Curative
Resection of diseased tissue
Pulmonary resection (pneumonectomy, lobectomy, segmentectomy) for destroyed lung, cavitary DR-TB, or localised MDR-TB with viable remaining lung
🚨
Emergency / Life-saving
Haemoptysis, tension pneumothorax
Emergency surgery for massive haemoptysis unresponsive to bronchial artery embolisation (BAE); drainage of tension pneumothorax; emergency decortication for empyema
🔧
Reconstructive / Palliative
Sequelae management
Thoracoplasty for residual space problems; decortication for trapped lung; bronchoplasty for bronchial stenosis; pleurodesis for recurrent pneumothorax
💧
Drainage Procedures
Empyema, abscess
Tube thoracostomy / open drainage for TB empyema; cavernostomy (open drainage of cavity) for massive infected cavities in poor surgical candidates
🔬
DR-TB Adjunct
MDR / XDR-TB
Surgery + chemotherapy improves culture conversion and treatment success in selected MDR/XDR-TB patients with localised, resectable disease who are failing medical therapy

Relative Indications for Surgery

  • Persistently open cavity after adequate ATT (6 months): Large cavities (>2 cm) that fail to close; especially in MDR-TB; harbour smear-positive organisms; risk of reactivation and haemoptysis
  • Tuberculoma: Cannot be distinguished from malignancy; growing on serial CT; patient preference; size >3 cm with indeterminate features
  • Recurrent haemoptysis (non-massive): Repeated episodes not amenable to BAE; from bronchiectatic segment or Rasmussen's aneurysm
  • Localised MDR/XDR-TB with culture conversion failure: Focal unilateral disease; adequate pulmonary reserve; surgery as adjunct to chemotherapy significantly improves outcomes
  • Secondary bacterial infection / lung abscess: Post-TB bronchiectasis with recurrent infections unresponsive to antibiotics; localised destroyed segment
  • Pericardial effusion causing tamponade: TB pericarditis with haemodynamic compromise → emergency pericardiocentesis or surgical pericardiectomy for constrictive pericarditis (late sequela)
  • Aspergilloma (mycetoma) in post-TB cavity: Symptomatic aspergilloma in residual TB cavity causing haemoptysis — surgery is definitive treatment; medical antifungals are adjuncts only
  • Diagnostic uncertainty — cannot exclude malignancy: Pulmonary nodule/mass in post-TB patient with suspicious CT features where bronchoscopy and CT-guided biopsy are non-diagnostic
Rasmussen's Aneurysm Pseudoaneurysm of pulmonary artery branch within or adjacent to a TB cavity — caused by erosion of vessel wall by the tuberculous process. Presents as catastrophic haemoptysis. Management: Bronchial artery embolisation (BAE) as emergency; followed by surgical resection of the involved segment/lobe
06

Contraindications to Surgery

Absolute Contraindications

  • Bilateral extensive / diffuse parenchymal disease
  • Severely impaired lung function: FEV₁ <40% predicted OR predicted post-op FEV₁ <800 ml
  • Active uncontrolled TB without adequate chemotherapy coverage
  • Uncontrolled coagulopathy or haematological disorder
  • Recent myocardial infarction (<6 weeks) or decompensated cardiac failure
  • Severe pulmonary hypertension (mPAP >45 mmHg)

Relative Contraindications

  • FEV₁ 40–60% predicted — high risk; careful selection
  • Contralateral lung disease reducing reserve
  • Sputum smear-positive status (ideally achieve negativity before elective surgery)
  • Severe malnutrition (albumin <2.5 g/dL) — nutritional optimisation first
  • Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus
  • HIV with low CD4 (<200 cells/mm³) — increased operative risk
  • Age >70 years with poor performance status
Key Pulmonary Function Criteria (Before Resection) Minimum acceptable pre-operative pulmonary function: FEV₁ >2 L (or >60% predicted) for pneumonectomy; FEV₁ >1.5 L for lobectomy; FEV₁ >1 L for lesser resections. Predicted post-operative FEV₁ (ppoFEV₁) must be >800 ml or >40% predicted. DLCO <40% = very high risk

Non-resectional & Collapse Procedures

🔩
Thoracoplasty
Rib resection to collapse lung
Removal of multiple ribs (usually 3–7) to permanently collapse the chest wall inward → compress the underlying diseased lung. Used for: residual pleural space post-pneumonectomy, TB empyema with BPF, or as an adjunct to collapse a persistent cavity in a patient who cannot tolerate resection. Historically the main TB surgery; now rarely performed
🩹
Decortication
Removal of pleural peel
Removal of thickened fibrous pleural cortex (peel) to allow trapped lung to re-expand. Indicated for: organised TB empyema causing fibrothorax; chronic empyema with trapped lung; post-TB pleural thickening causing restrictive physiology. Timing: ideally 4–6 weeks after empyema develops (before calcification)
🚰
Cavernostomy (Open Drainage)
Direct cavity drainage
Open surgical drainage of a TB cavity (Monaldi procedure). Indicated when: massive cavity in poor surgical candidate; fungal ball (aspergilloma) in TB cavity causing haemoptysis; not amenable to resection. Usually a temporising measure; definitive resection later if patient's condition improves
🌊
Open Window Thoracostomy (Eloesser Flap)
Chronic drainage of empyema
Creation of a permanent opening in the chest wall for open drainage of a chronic TB empyema — especially when patient is too unwell for definitive decortication or when BPF is present. A "last resort" for infected pleural space
💨
Pleurodesis
Obliteration of pleural space
Chemical (talc / bleomycin) or surgical (VATS abrasion) pleurodesis for: recurrent TB-related pneumothorax; persistent air leak. Prevents recurrence by obliterating the pleural space
🎯
Bronchial Artery Embolisation (BAE)
Interventional — not surgical but key adjunct
Catheter-based embolisation of bronchial arteries supplying the bleeding point. First-line for massive haemoptysis in TB. Success rate ~80–90% initially; 30–50% recurrence requiring repeat BAE or surgery. Always followed by definitive surgery if patient is operable

Post-operative Care & Complications

Post-operative Care

  • ICU monitoring post-pneumonectomy for 24–48 hours
  • Intercostal drains — monitor for air leak, bleeding, chyle
  • Continue ATT for planned full duration post-op
  • Early mobilisation and chest physiotherapy
  • Infection control — airborne precautions if still smear-positive
  • Nutritional support — high protein diet; Nikshay Poshan Yojana
  • Regular sputum follow-up cultures (especially MDR-TB)

Complications

  • Bronchopleural fistula (BPF) — most serious; 5–10% post-pneumonectomy; presents as sudden air leak or empyema
  • Empyema — infected pleural space post-resection
  • Post-pneumonectomy syndrome — mediastinal shift, dysphagia, haemoptysis
  • Respiratory failure — especially with borderline pre-op function
  • Prolonged air leak — >5 days; from alveolar pleural fistula
  • Haemorrhage — intraoperative or early post-op bleeding
  • Wound infection — especially in malnourished TB patients
  • ARDS — post-pneumonectomy pulmonary oedema

Surgery in Drug-Resistant TB (MDR / XDR-TB)

Why Surgery in DR-TB? MDR-TB treatment success with chemotherapy alone is only 58–60% globally. In XDR-TB, success rates may be as low as 30–40%. Surgery removes the bulk of resistant bacilli, reduces bacterial load, eliminates cavities (reservoirs for drug-resistant organisms), and improves culture conversion rates. Surgery is an adjunct — never a replacement — to optimised chemotherapy
  • Best candidates for surgery in MDR-TB: Unilateral or localised bilateral disease · Adequate contralateral lung function · Sputum culture conversion failure despite ≥4 months of optimised regimen · Resistant to ≥4 drugs but sensitive to at least 2–3 effective drugs for pre/post-op coverage
  • Surgery timing in MDR-TB: Ideally after culture conversion (usually 2–6 months of effective regimen) for elective cases; emergency surgery for life-threatening haemoptysis at any time
  • Preferred procedure: Lobectomy for localised MDR-TB; pneumonectomy for destroyed lung; avoid bilateral pneumonectomy
  • Duration of post-operative ATT: Continue for minimum 12–18 months post-surgery for MDR-TB; WHO recommends 15–21 months total for MDR-TB regimens
  • New drugs compatibility: Bedaquiline and Linezolid — compatible with surgery; no interactions with anaesthetic agents; continue throughout peri-operative period
